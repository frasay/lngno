01 / The Crisis

THE 2024
ENERGY SHOCK

In August 2024, wholesale electricity prices in New Zealand hit $800 per megawatt-hour—a price so extreme it threatened businesses, drove up consumer bills, and exposed deep structural failures in the energy system.

The cause wasn't mysterious. It was a perfect storm: declining domestic gas production collided with low hydro lake inflows during a dry winter. The gas-fired power stations that normally cover these dry periods simply didn't have enough fuel.

Gas reserves had already dropped by 109 petajoules in recent revisions. The system that once had comfortable margins was running on empty.

02 / The Diagnosis

THREE
FAULTLINES

The Sapere report identifies three structural weaknesses in New Zealand's energy system. Each is distinct. Each needs a different fix. LNG addresses none of them well.

01

GAS CAN'T FLEX ANYMORE

Gas-fired power stations used to ramp up in dry years to cover low hydro generation. But declining production capacity means gas can no longer play this role.

Faultline 01: The Flexibility Gap

Gas production from fields like Pohokura and Maui has been declining steadily. Recent reserve revisions removed 109PJ from known reserves. The remaining 731PJ must serve both electricity generation (25PJ/year) and industrial/commercial use (55PJ/year).

In the past, gas-fired generators could increase output by 3–4 TWh in a dry year. That flexibility is disappearing. By the early 2030s, it may be gone entirely.

The real solution: Renewable overbuild (wind and solar) combined with diesel as a short-term backup fuel. Diesel peakers can be online within 2 years; new wind farms within 3–5 years.

Faultline 02: Industrial Gas Dependency

New Zealand's industrial sector uses approximately 55PJ of gas per year for process heat—everything from dairy processing to timber drying to food manufacturing.

As gas supply contracts, these industries face deindustrialisation by the late 2030s unless they switch to alternative fuels. 88% of electrification projects need no network upgrades at all.

The real solution: Accelerated fuel switching to electricity, biomass, and geothermal, supported by $958M in targeted grants. This is cheaper than LNG and creates permanent energy cost reductions.

Faultline 03: The Missing Market

New Zealand lacks an exchange-listed market for long-term, firm electricity contracts. Businesses that need price certainty—data centres, manufacturers, exporters—cannot hedge their exposure.

This isn't a fuel problem; it's a market design problem. No amount of LNG fixes it. What's needed is a neutral entity or mandatory trigger mechanism to create firm-energy contracts.

The real solution: Establish an exchange-traded firm-energy contract market, potentially with mandatory participation triggers, so businesses can manage risk regardless of fuel source.

05 / The Better Path

WHAT SHOULD
HAPPEN INSTEAD.

The Sapere report outlines a three-part strategy that's faster, cheaper, and actually solves all three faultlines.

A

Short Term · 2025–2028

DIESEL AS A BRIDGE FUEL

Convert existing gas peaker plants to dual-fuel capability. This can be done in 1–2 years at minimal cost (~$100M). Diesel is more expensive per unit than gas, but it's available immediately, has reliable global supply chains, and requires no new import infrastructure.

Diesel peakers would only run during genuine dry-year emergencies—perhaps a few hundred hours per year. The total fuel cost is manageable and acts as insurance, not baseload generation.

B

Medium Term · 2026–2033

RENEWABLE OVERBUILD FOR DRY YEARS

Build approximately 800MW of new wind generation specifically designed to provide firm energy during dry years. The capital cost is around $2.6 billion, but the fuel cost is zero—forever.

By 2033, this combination of diesel bridge + renewables is approximately $900 million cheaper than the LNG pathway, while also reducing emissions and eliminating dependence on volatile international gas markets.

C

Parallel Track · 2025–2035

ACCELERATE INDUSTRIAL FUEL SWITCHING

Provide $958 million in targeted grants to help industrial users switch from gas to electricity, biomass, or geothermal for process heat. 88% of electrification projects need no network upgrades.

This directly addresses Faultline 2—industrial gas dependency—which LNG would actually worsen by encouraging continued reliance on fossil fuels.

+

Structural Reform

CREATE A FIRM-ENERGY CONTRACT MARKET

Establish an exchange-traded market for long-term, firm-priced electricity contracts. This is a market design fix that no fuel choice—LNG or otherwise—can substitute for.

Without this, businesses will remain exposed to spot market volatility regardless of how electricity is generated.

LNG DOESN'T FIX
ANY OF THE THREE
FAULTLINES WELL.

It partially addresses dry-year firming (Faultline 1) but at far higher cost than alternatives. It actively worsens industrial gas dependency (Faultline 2). And it does nothing for the missing contract market (Faultline 3).

06 / The Timeline

TIME IS THE
CRITICAL FACTOR.

LNG requires 5–7 years to build import infrastructure. Diesel conversion takes 1–2 years. Renewables take 3–5 years. The next dry year could come anytime.
